# Day 3: Save Your Work the Safe Way

You've got a business brain now. Let's make sure you never lose it.

## The fear nobody talks about

Everybody who works on a computer has the same quiet fear: what if I lose it? What if the file gets deleted, or the laptop dies, or I change something and wish I could go back? Today kills that fear for good.

There's a simple habit that keeps your work safe, backed up online, and even lets you rewind to an earlier version if you ever need to. It sounds technical, but you're going to find it's almost effortless.

## Two words, explained simply

There are two words you'll hear: "commit" and "push." Here's all they mean.

A commit is like hitting save with a little sticky note attached. The note says what you changed, like "filled in my business brain." Think of it as a snapshot, a frozen picture of your work at this exact moment. You can always come back to any snapshot later.

A push sends that snapshot up to GitHub, your free online backup. GitHub is like Google Drive for projects like this. Once you've pushed, your work lives safely online. If your computer fell in a lake tomorrow, your work would still be there.

So: commit saves a snapshot, push backs it up online. That's the entire idea. Together, people just call it "saving your work."

## The good news: you don't type any commands

In a lot of tutorials, this is where they make you memorize scary commands in a black terminal window. Not here.

You just tell Claude "save my work." Claude does the commit and the push for you, picks a sensible note to describe what changed, and tells you plainly what it did. That's it. No commands, no memorizing.

## Make it a habit

This is the one habit worth building from day one. Any time you finish something you'd be sad to lose, a filled-in file, a new skill, a batch of content, just say "save my work." Five seconds, and it's safe.

A good rhythm is to save at the end of each work session. Finished for now? "Save my work." Done.
